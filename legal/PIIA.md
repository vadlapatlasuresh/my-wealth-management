# PROPRIETARY INFORMATION AND INVENTIONS AGREEMENT
### (IP Assignment Agreement — Developers and Contractors)

> **NOT LEGAL ADVICE.** This is a drafting starting point prepared for review by licensed counsel.
>
> **READ THIS BEFORE USING.** This document has more legal landmines than the NDA. Three in particular:
>
> 1. **The state-statute carve-out in Section 3.4 is mandatory in several states.** In California, Delaware, Illinois, Kansas, Minnesota, New Jersey, North Carolina, Utah, Washington, and others, an invention-assignment provision that does not give the required written notice of the statutory carve-out is **unenforceable as to that provision** — and in California, failure to give notice is itself a violation. Do not delete Section 3.4 or Exhibit B.
> 2. **Choice of law will not save you.** If your contractor sits in California, a Delaware choice-of-law clause will not reliably displace California Labor Code §§ 2870–2872 or § 16600. Section 12.1 must be set to the right state, and Section 8 must be adjusted or deleted for California, Washington, Minnesota, and other states restricting non-competes and customer non-solicits.
> 3. **"Work made for hire" does not do what people think for contractors.** See Section 3.2 and the explanation notes. The assignment language — not the work-for-hire language — is what actually transfers ownership from an independent contractor. Both are included, in the correct order.
>
> **Worker classification.** If you use this for an "independent contractor" who in practice works set hours, uses your equipment, takes direction on how to do the work, and works only for you, calling them a contractor in Section 10 will not make them one. Misclassification exposure (back taxes, penalties, benefits) is real and is a common startup diligence finding. Get this classification right before you sign, not after.

## 3. ASSIGNMENT OF INVENTIONS

**3.1 Present Assignment.** You **hereby irrevocably assign, transfer, and convey** to the Company, and shall be deemed to have assigned automatically upon creation, your entire worldwide right, title, and interest in and to all Company Inventions and all Intellectual Property Rights in them, effective immediately upon their creation, conception, authorship, or reduction to practice, without further consideration.

> *Drafting note: the present-tense "hereby assign" is deliberate and load-bearing. A promise to assign in the future ("agrees to assign") has been held to create only an equitable claim that can be defeated by a later assignee — see* Stanford v. Roche*, 563 U.S. 776 (2011), and* FilmTec v. Allied Signal*, 939 F.2d 1568 (Fed. Cir. 1991). Do not soften this language.*

**3.2 Work Made for Hire.** You acknowledge that all Company Inventions that are works of authorship and that qualify under 17 U.S.C. § 101 are "**works made for hire**" owned by the Company from the moment of creation. **To the extent any Company Invention does not so qualify** — including because you are an independent contractor and the work does not fall within one of the nine statutory categories of specially ordered or commissioned works — **Section 3.1 governs and operates as a present, outright assignment of that Company Invention and all Intellectual Property Rights in it.**

> *Drafting note: for independent contractors, the work-for-hire doctrine usually does* not *apply. Software is not one of the nine enumerated categories under § 101, and* CCNV v. Reid*, 490 U.S. 730 (1989), makes clear that a contractor's work is not "for hire" merely because a contract says so. The assignment in 3.1 is what actually moves ownership. This Section is a backstop, not the primary mechanism.*

**3.3 Disclosure.** You shall promptly disclose to the Company in writing all Company Inventions. You shall also disclose all Inventions you make during the Engagement that you believe are **not** Company Inventions, solely so the Company can determine whether it agrees. **Any disclosure under this second sentence shall be received in confidence by the Company, shall not be used by the Company for any purpose other than that determination, and shall not itself effect any assignment.** If you are in California, you may satisfy this by disclosing only enough information to permit the determination, and you need not disclose information you reasonably believe to be a trade secret of a third party.

**3.4 Statutory Exclusion — READ THIS.** **This Agreement does not apply to, and you do not assign, any Invention that qualifies for protection under the applicable state statute set forth in Exhibit B.** In general, and subject to the exact text of the applicable statute, this means any Invention that you developed **entirely on your own time without using the Company's equipment, supplies, facilities, or trade secret information**, and that **either** (a) does not relate at the time of conception or reduction to practice to the Company's business or to its actual or demonstrably anticipated research or development, **or** (b) does not result from any work you performed for the Company. **Exhibit B is incorporated into this Agreement and constitutes the written notice required by the applicable statute.** To the extent any provision of this Agreement conflicts with the applicable statute, **the statute controls.**

**3.5 Moral Rights Waiver.** To the fullest extent permitted by applicable law, you **irrevocably waive and agree never to assert** any and all moral rights and rights of a similar nature in the Company Inventions — including rights of attribution, integrity, disclosure, and withdrawal, and rights under the Visual Artists Rights Act (17 U.S.C. § 106A) and any comparable law in any jurisdiction — against the Company, its successors, assigns, and licensees. **Where such rights cannot be waived as a matter of law, you irrevocably consent to any act or omission by the Company or its licensees that would otherwise infringe them, and agree not to institute or support any claim or proceeding alleging their infringement or violation.**

> *Drafting note: moral rights are non-waivable in much of Europe and in some other jurisdictions. The consent-and-covenant-not-to-assert fallback in the second sentence is what carries the clause where waiver fails. If your contractor is outside the US, flag this for counsel.*

**3.6 Further Assurances.** During and after the Engagement, you shall promptly execute all documents and take all actions the Company reasonably requests, **at the Company's expense**, to perfect, register, record, defend, or enforce its rights in the Company Inventions anywhere in the world. **[REVIEW WITH COUNSEL: If the Company requests such assistance more than [INSERT, e.g., twelve (12)] months after the Engagement ends, the Company shall compensate you at a reasonable hourly rate of [INSERT RATE] for time actually spent, in addition to expenses.]**

## 8. NON-COMPETITION — READ BEFORE INCLUDING

**[STRONGLY RECOMMEND DELETING THIS SECTION. DISCUSS WITH COUNSEL BEFORE INCLUDING.]**

> **Why this is bracketed.** Non-competes against independent contractors are void or sharply limited in a growing number of states — including California (Bus. & Prof. Code § 16600, and § 16600.5, which reaches contracts signed elsewhere and creates a private right of action against employers who merely *attempt* to enforce), Washington, Oklahoma, North Dakota, Minnesota, and, for many workers, Colorado, Illinois, Oregon, and others. Several states impose penalties or fee-shifting for presenting an unenforceable one. The practical cost of including it is real and the practical benefit is usually near zero: **Sections 2 and 3, plus trade-secret law, already protect what you actually care about.** A non-compete also makes strong developers walk away, and it is a recurring diligence flag.
>
> If you have a genuine, narrow need — a senior architect with full system access, in a state that permits it, for a short period, with consideration — have counsel draft it to that state's specific requirements. Do not use a generic clause here.

> **§ 2870.** (a) Any provision in an employment agreement which provides that an employee shall assign, or offer to assign, any of his or her rights in an invention to his or her employer shall not apply to an invention that the employee developed entirely on his or her own time without using the employer's equipment, supplies, facilities, or trade secret information except for those inventions that either:
>
> (1) Relate at the time of conception or reduction to practice of the invention to the employer's business, or actual or demonstrably anticipated research or development of the employer; or

> (2) Result from any work performed by the employee for the employer.
